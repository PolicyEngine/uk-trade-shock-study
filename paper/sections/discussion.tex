\section{Discussion and conclusion}\label{sec:discussion}

This paper asked who bears the 2025 US tariffs on UK goods after the tax--benefit system responds. Three conclusions stand.

First, the adjustment margin, not the tariff rate alone, determines what the shock \emph{is} when it reaches households---but the determination runs through the \emph{reliability} of the state's response rather than its size. The UK tax--benefit system cushions an in-work wage cut and an earnings-equivalent wave of job losses to a similar degree---41.0 per cent against $38.9 \pm 3.7$ at the central take-up assumption---and reaches those similar totals by opposite instruments. Wage cuts are relieved almost entirely through income tax, at marginal rates, automatically, with no claim to make and no gate to pass; the rate is exactly invariant to benefit take-up. Job losses are relieved by a smaller income-tax offset, at average rates, topped up by Universal Credit, which about half of displaced workers' families never receive because of the joint partner-income taper or because they do not claim; that rate moves some seven points across the plausible take-up range while its income-tax component does not move at all. Statutory generosity toward the unemployed is not economic generosity until the gates are cleared---and whether they are cleared is not something the shock, or the schedule, determines. An earlier version of this paper reported an eight-point level gap in favour of the wage-cut margin; that gap was an artefact of carrying a pre-shock claiming flag through the shock, is retracted in Section~\ref{sec:results-cushioning}, and the corrected difference is small and not separately identified from draw noise. The fiscal picture is the mirror: the two margins cost the Exchequer almost the same (\pounds 0.99 billion against \pounds 0.92 $\pm$ 0.31 billion), a difference far inside one draw-to-draw SD---while only the displacement margin moves poverty at all.

Second, on the displacement margin the shock is regionally, not vertically, concentrated. Displaced manufacturing earnings sit in deciles 7--10, so national inequality and poverty statistics barely register a shock that is nonetheless a \pounds 100--200-per-head event in whichever handful of regions a given realisation lands on. Trade-shock incidence in the UK is a geography problem wearing the clothes of a distribution problem---consistent with the China-shock literature's regional persistence findings \citep{autor2021, dixcarneirokovak2017} but established here at the household, rules-based level.

Third, the Economic Prosperity Deal was worth about $6{,}500$ jobs and roughly \pounds 0.2 billion a year to the Exchequer on the direct channel---real but modest, concentrated in autos, with the pharmaceutical clause's true cost routed through the NHS rather than the labour market and the steel clause largely beside the sector's EU-dominated exposure.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Limitations.} Seven are worth stating plainly. (1) The inactivity margin assumes every displaced worker aged 50 and over passes the Work Capability Assessment, which is deliberately an upper bound on the health-element route; no assessment process, waiting period or conditionality regime is modelled, though the WCA is a gatekeeping institution whose pass rates respond to local labour-market slack \citep{robertstaylor2022} and the health-related caseload was already at a post-pandemic high when the tariffs arrived \citep{ifs2024}. Realised flows could sit anywhere between the 50-per-cent sensitivity variant and the upper bound. (2) Monte Carlo dispersion is large relative to the means, so only orderings that survive it are asserted; the displacement--wage-cut fiscal gap is identified by the aggregate cushioning accounting rather than by the draws. (3) Absolute BHC poverty against a fixed baseline line is computed outside the model, which carries no such variable in this version. (4) The central analysis covers the direct exposed-sector earnings channel only. Upstream propagation---which the production-network literature finds can multiply direct effects severalfold \citep{barrotsauvagnat2016, acemoglu2016}---is bounded in Appendix~\ref{app:supplychain}, where a single Leontief round roughly doubles the gross earnings loss and leaves every structural ordering intact. Still omitted are downstream and induced-consumption rounds, local-multiplier ripple effects put at 0.4--1.6 additional local jobs per lost tradable job \citep{moretti2010, gathmann2020}, general-equilibrium wage responses, the exchange-rate offset \citep{corsettilloydostry2025}, and retaliation; fiscal policy is held fixed throughout, and \citet{ignatenko2025} show revenue recycling can flip aggregate employment signs, so the fiscal numbers are first-round incidence, not equilibrium outcomes. The direct shock is accordingly a lower bound: compounding the upstream round with local multipliers implies an all-channel figure of roughly 0.16--0.31 per cent of GDP, approaching the OBR's one-third to three-quarters of a per cent from below. (5) No behavioural margin is active on the household side. The reallocation family does move workers between sectors, answering the objection that after the China shock displaced manufacturing workers went into healthcare, delivery and retail rather than staying unemployed \citep{autor2014, dauth2021}; but who reallocates is \emph{assumed} rather than estimated, no worker reallocates partially or fails to reallocate, and the sector switch is fiscally inert, so all the economic content sits in the earnings and hours penalty. Added-worker responses, retirement timing, self-employment entry \citep{irastorza2025} and labour-supply responses to the tax and benefit changes themselves are all suppressed. (6) The constituency layer is illustrative only: its industry codes are imputed on a different dataset and period, so it cannot reproduce observed local industry mix, and its seat ranking should not be read against exposure-based analyses that can \citep{cityredi2025, centreforcities2025}. (7) The trade numerator is on the HMRC customs basis, which sits below the ONS balance-of-payments basis; intensities inherit that coverage gap. (8) Take-up among the newly entitled displaced is \emph{assumed}, not observed. The central runs re-draw the Universal Credit claiming flag at 0.80 among affected benefit units (Section~\ref{sec:method-takeup}), a convention borrowed from the applied UK literature rather than an estimate: DWP has published no take-up estimate for Universal Credit at all, and the first is not due until October 2026. Nor would a steady-state take-up rate be the right object even once it exists---the relevant quantity is take-up among families newly entitled through involuntary job loss, who differ from the standing caseload in exactly the ways (recent earnings, partner in work, no claiming history) that plausibly depress take-up. The paper's response is to bound rather than to assert: Table~\ref{tab:takeupgrid} reports the whole 0.55--1.00 grid, the displacement cushioning rate spans 35.5 to 41.5 per cent across it, and every claim made in this paper is either invariant to the parameter (the wage-cut margin, the income-tax component, the composition contrast) or is stated with the grid attached.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Further work.} Beyond modelling the WCA gate itself, three directions: a measured-margin family calibrated to the accumulating outturn, replacing the stylised poles with the mix of displacement and wage cuts the data actually chose, in the spirit of \citet{rud2022}'s decomposition; sharpening the constituency geography with observed local industry structure (BRES employment by SIC) in place of the demographic imputation; and dynamic transition paths---re-employment hazards, scarring, and the multi-year fiscal tail that \citet{dixcarneiro2014} and \citet{beatty2017} both imply a one-year snapshot understates.

The bottom line is not where the paper began. The 2025 tariff reaches UK households mainly as a public-finance event, not a poverty event; and the two arms of the welfare state that absorb it---the tax schedule and the means-tested safety net---turn out to absorb it about equally well, but not equally dependably. The one that requires nothing of the household delivers what it delivers regardless of behaviour; the one built for the jobless delivers an amount that depends on gates being cleared and claims being made. On the evidence here that is the more consequential distinction between the two adjustment margins, and it is the one a policy designer can act on.
