\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

All results are computed on Family Resources Survey 2024--25 microdata for the 2026 period, on the HBAI cash disposable income concept throughout (Section~\ref{sec:method-microsim}). Displacement and inactivity results are means over 50 Monte Carlo draws (the reallocation family over 20 paired draws) with $\pm$ values denoting standard deviations across draws; the wage-cut margin is deterministic. The take-up grid and the demographic, poverty-gap and reallocation-variant tables use 20 draws (seeds 0--19), as noted at each. Decile and regional profiles are Monte Carlo means across the 50 displacement draws. Because the draw-to-draw standard deviation on the Exchequer cost is large (about \pounds 0.31 billion against means of \pounds 0.7--1.0 billion), I narrate only orderings and contrasts that survive it, and flag those that do not. All central runs re-draw the Universal Credit take-up flag for benefit units containing a newly affected person at a take-up rate of 0.80 among the newly entitled (Section~\ref{sec:method-takeup}); Section~\ref{sec:results-cushioning} reports the full grid, which is a finding rather than a robustness check.

I lead with the paper's headline finding. Section~\ref{sec:method-margins} argued---as the naive reading of Universal Credit suggests---that the tax--benefit system should replace a job loss more generously than an in-work wage cut of equal aggregate size. On the FRS microdata the two margins are cushioned to a \emph{similar} degree: $38.9 \pm 3.7$ per cent of the gross earnings loss when the shock arrives as displacement, against 41.0 per cent when the identical aggregate loss arrives as proportional wage cuts. The difference is about two points, well inside one draw-to-draw standard deviation, and I do not claim it as an ordering. What is sharp, robust and new is the \emph{composition} of that similar total, and it is the finding this paper is built on. Wage cuts are cushioned overwhelmingly by income tax (35.2 of the 41.0 points), with Universal Credit contributing 1.5; displacement draws 21.2 points from income tax and 9.5 from Universal Credit. Because a proportional pay cut creates no new claimants, the wage-cut rate is \emph{exactly} invariant to benefit take-up, while the displacement rate swings some seven points across the plausible take-up range---and its income-tax component does not move at all. Wage-cut protection is therefore automatic and mechanical: it arrives through the tax schedule whether or not anyone acts. Job-loss protection is contingent: it requires clearing eligibility gates and actually claiming. The adjustment margin determines not just \emph{where} the shock lands and how much the state absorbs, but how \emph{reliably} the absorption happens.

The anchor check. Applied to FRS 2024--25 employees, the full schedule removes \pounds 1.77 billion of gross annual earnings---about 0.06 per cent of GDP. This is the \emph{direct exposed-sector earnings channel only}. Under the EPD schedule the gross earnings loss is \pounds 1.39 billion.

It is worth putting the paper's three aggregates on one line, as shares of GDP, against the macro benchmark. The direct exposed-sector earnings channel is 0.06 per cent (full schedule); the measured-outturn family, which imposes no elasticity at all, is \pounds 0.95 billion, about 0.03 per cent (Section~\ref{sec:results-measured}); and the supply-chain-amplified shock, adding one upstream Leontief round, is \pounds 3.45 billion, about 0.12 per cent (Appendix~\ref{app:supplychain}). The OBR's March 2025 analysis puts the \emph{permanent} UK GDP loss from a 20-point US tariff at one-third to three-quarters of a per cent, reaching roughly 1 per cent at peak in its reciprocal-tariff scenario \citep{obr2025}. Our figures sit below that range, as they should: the OBR's is an all-channel general-equilibrium estimate including uncertainty, investment and trade-diversion channels a static microsimulation deliberately excludes. The gap is smaller than it looks, however, because the omitted channels are broadly \emph{additive} rather than alternative: the upstream Leontief round is a factor of 1.95 on the direct channel, and local-multiplier estimates of 0.4--1.6 additional local jobs per lost tradable job \citep{moretti2010, gathmann2020} are a further factor of roughly 1.4--2.6 on top of it. The two factors compound without double-counting because the 1.95 is Type-I-equivalent---direct plus indirect upstream requirements, with no induced-consumption round---so the induced channel enters only once, through the local multipliers. For scale, ONS publishes Type I output multipliers for manufacturing of roughly 1.4--2.0, and 1.95 sits at the top of that range, as one would expect for the input-intensive exposed manufacturing divisions rather than manufacturing as a whole. Compounding accordingly gives an all-channel figure of about 2.7--5.1 times the direct earnings shock---0.16 to 0.31 per cent of GDP---approaches the OBR range from below, with investment, uncertainty and general-equilibrium price channels still to add. The elasticity itself is anchored on the realised outturn rather than borrowed from the literature: UK goods exports to the US fell 24.7 per cent in April 2025 against a trade-weighted average full-schedule tariff of 12.8 per cent computed from Table~\ref{tab:shocks}'s primitives, giving $0.247/0.128 = 1.93$, rounded to 2.0---also mid-range of short-run trade elasticities (Section~\ref{sec:results-validation}). Because the elasticity and the pass-through enter the derived shock only through their product, the pound levels above scale approximately linearly in $\varepsilon\pi$ while every rate and ordering in this section is essentially invariant to it: Appendix~\ref{app:grid} verifies this on a $4 \times 3$ grid over $\varepsilon \in \{1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0\}$ and $\pi \in \{0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$, which also serves as the uncertainty band around the one-month outturn anchor.

Realised as displacement, the full schedule puts $34{,}200$ workers ($\pm 3{,}400$ across 50 draws) out of work; the EPD schedule $27{,}700$ ($\pm 4{,}800$; the quota expectations are $33{,}566$ and $26{,}709$). The age profile of the displaced skews old relative to the workforce---across draws, $17 \pm 10$ per cent are aged 55--64 and roughly a quarter are 55 or over---which is what gives the inactivity margin its bite: $12{,}400$ of the $34{,}200$ displaced are 50 or over and flow to benefit-financed inactivity with the UC health element (Section~\ref{sec:results-margins}).

\subsection{Cushioning: similar totals, opposite instruments}\label{sec:results-cushioning}

Table~\ref{tab:cushioning} makes the level result precise. Define the cushioning rate as one minus the ratio of the aggregate household disposable income loss to the aggregate gross earnings loss. Over 50 Monte Carlo draws the full schedule's displacement margin is cushioned $38.7 \pm 4.1$ per cent against the earnings-equivalent wage cut's 41.0 per cent; under the EPD schedule, $37.9 \pm 4.9$ against 40.8. On the 20-draw grid that holds the take-up assumption explicit the same comparison is $38.9 \pm 3.7$ against 41.0 (full) and $37.8 \pm 5.1$ against 40.8 (EPD). The two margins are cushioned to a similar degree, and the two-to-three-point difference sits well inside one draw-to-draw standard deviation.

\paragraph{A retraction.} An earlier treatment of this material reported the displacement margin cushioned at 32.8--33 per cent against the wage cut's 41.0---an eight-point gap presented as a reversal of the standard intensive/extensive ordering. That gap is retracted. It was an artefact of the modelled treatment of Universal Credit take-up, in two parts. First, PolicyEngine's benefit-unit \texttt{would\_claim\_uc} is a \emph{stored} flag drawn at dataset build time against a flat population-wide target of 0.55 over all benefit units---a share of all families, not take-up among the entitled (the realised flag rate is 0.549 over all FRS benefit units and 0.541 among UC-eligible ones). The earlier draft's ``55 per cent non-take-up'' therefore quoted a population parameter and was never comparable to the roughly 80 per cent take-up-among-entitled that the Resolution Foundation and the IFS assume. Second, and more consequentially, nothing re-drew that flag after the shock, so a displaced worker carried the claiming decision made while employed and, in most cases, not entitled: measured take-up among the displaced post-shock was 46.9 per cent, \emph{below} even the population flag rate. In plain terms, the old model had people not claiming after losing their job because they had not been claiming while they had one. The pipeline now re-draws the flag post-shock for every benefit unit containing a newly affected person, at a scenario parameter with default 0.80, on an independent random stream so the displacement draw itself is unchanged (Section~\ref{sec:method-takeup}). Under the corrected treatment the gap is about two points and is not separately identified from draw noise. The claim that the UK system cushions wage cuts \emph{more} than job losses does not survive, and is withdrawn.

\paragraph{The contingency result.} What replaces it is stronger, because it does not depend on the take-up assumption at all. Table~\ref{tab:takeupgrid} runs the whole grid: five take-up rates from 0.55 to 1.00, both tariff schedules, both margins, with the component split. Three features carry the paper. First, the displacement cushioning rate moves substantially with claiming behaviour---35.5 to 41.5 per cent under the full schedule, 33.5 to 40.3 under the EPD, a swing of roughly six to seven points---and all of that movement is the Universal Credit component, which runs from 6.2 to 12.1 points (full) and 5.6 to 12.4 (EPD). Second, the income-tax component of displacement cushioning is \emph{constant to the reported precision at every take-up rate}: 21.2 points under the full schedule and 19.7 under the EPD, in every row. Take-up cannot touch it, because the tax relief is arithmetic on a destroyed earnings stream. Third, the wage-cut margin is exactly invariant: its cushioning rate is 41.0 (full) and 40.8 (EPD) at every rate on the grid, with a verified maximum spread of 0.0, because a proportional pay cut moves no family from non-entitlement into entitlement and so triggers no re-draw.

The reading is a statement about the \emph{reliability} of insurance rather than its generosity. The UK tax--benefit system protects the two adjustment margins to a similar extent, but it protects them by different machinery with different failure modes. Wage-cut protection is automatic and mechanical: it operates through the income-tax schedule, requires no claim, passes no gate, and is invariant to how many people claim. Job-loss protection is contingent: roughly a quarter of it runs through a means-tested benefit that a family must be entitled to and must actually claim, so the realised degree of protection depends on behaviour the modeller cannot observe and the state does not control. A shock that arrives as job losses is insured about as generously as one that arrives as pay cuts, and considerably less dependably.

It is worth being precise about how this sits against \citet{dolls2012}, the closest existing benchmark, because the corrected treatment brings us into agreement with it. Their UK-specific cells (Appendix Tables 2--3) give a stabilisation coefficient of 0.352 for a proportional income shock---income tax 0.267, social insurance contributions 0.054, benefits 0.031---and 0.415 for an unemployment shock, of which income tax 0.191, SIC 0.061 and out-of-work benefits 0.163. Our 41.0 per cent on the wage-cut margin against their 35.2, and our 38.9 per cent on displacement against their 41.5, are close on both margins---computed a decade apart, on different data, with different software. The earlier draft had to explain a fourfold discrepancy in the benefit component (0.163 against 0.042) and did so by appeal to their full-take-up convention; that reconciliation is no longer available and is withdrawn, because our own corrected treatment now assumes near-full take-up among the newly entitled. Our UC term at the central assumption is 9.5 points against their 16.3, and what remains of that residual is best attributed to composition: the population this shock displaces is above-median manufacturing earners, disproportionately in couples with a partner in work, on whom the joint partner-income taper bites hard (Section~\ref{sec:results-mechanism}). The general methodological claim the earlier draft drew from this comparison---that full-entitlement microsimulation systematically overstates extensive-margin stabilisation---rested on the retracted gap and is withdrawn with it. The narrower and better-supported statement is that the extensive margin's stabilisation is \emph{contingent} on a take-up parameter the intensive margin does not have, so any cross-margin stabilisation comparison should report the grid rather than a point.

\subsection{The mechanism, demonstrated}\label{sec:results-mechanism}

The decomposition is the paper's central object, and it says something different from what a single total says. The two margins reach 39.7 and 41.0 per cent by almost disjoint routes. The wage-cut margin recovers 35.2 per cent of the gross loss in foregone income tax against 19.8 under displacement: a proportional wage cut shaves the \emph{top slice} of every affected worker's earnings, relieved at marginal rates, whereas displacement removes the \emph{entire} earnings stream---including the slice covered by the personal allowance and taxed at zero---so the tax system hands back only the average rate. That 15-point tax deficit on the displacement side is made up almost entirely by Universal Credit, which contributes 11.1 points at seed 0 (9.5 on the 20-draw mean) against 1.5 on the wage-cut margin. National Insurance contributes equally on both margins (4.5 versus 4.3 per cent; NI's flatter schedule means its marginal and average rates differ less), and the other-benefits row contributes nothing at all under displacement: new-style contribution-based JSA pays zero to the displaced in the model, a scope limit (JSA claims are not activated by the displacement transition) that makes the displacement figures, if anything, conservative.

The asymmetry that matters is therefore not in the totals but in the \emph{character} of the components. Income tax relief is unconditional: it is the arithmetic consequence of a smaller taxable income, and Table~\ref{tab:takeupgrid} shows it fixed at 21.2 points across every take-up assumption. The Universal Credit contribution is conditional twice over---the family must be entitled after the joint means test, and it must claim---and it is the entire source of the seven-point swing in displacement cushioning across the grid. A quarter of job-loss protection thus rests on behaviour, where none of wage-cut protection does.

Which gates bind? Counting displaced workers' benefit units post-shock at the central take-up assumption (seed 0): 49 per cent receive positive UC, averaging \pounds 12{,}100 a year conditional on receipt, and 51 per cent receive none (across seeds 0--4, $48 \pm 11$ per cent receive none). Decomposing the zero-UC share by the constraint that binds, as a share of all displaced workers: 26 per cent are in families whose joint means test tapers the maximum award to zero against a partner's earnings and unearned income ($29 \pm 13$ per cent across seeds 0--4), 25 per cent are in families modelled as not claiming ($18 \pm 5$ per cent across seeds), and---contrary to the mechanism an early draft asserted---0 per cent are excluded by the \pounds 16{,}000 capital limit. The ordering of the two live gates has reversed relative to the earlier draft, which is a direct consequence of the correction: once claiming is re-drawn on post-shock circumstances, the partner-income taper is the larger obstacle, and non-claiming falls to roughly the complement of the assumed 0.80 rate applied to the entitled. The capital result remains a data limitation rather than a finding about the rules: the plain FRS 2024--25 dataset carries no household capital, so the capital test \emph{cannot} bind in the model. A counterfactual rerun makes the point mechanically: neutralising the UC capital rules (raising the \pounds 16{,}000 limit and the \pounds 6{,}000 tariff-income threshold by five orders of magnitude, taper and work allowances unchanged) leaves the displacement cushioning rate identical to four decimal places (39.73 per cent).

The mechanism should be claimed carefully, because part of it is older than this paper. That the income tax stabilises at effective \emph{marginal} rates is the organising idea of \citet{auerbachfeenberg2000}; \citet{mckayreis2016} formalise how progressivity and the intensive/extensive structure of a shock determine stabilisation in general equilibrium; and \citet{dolls2012} explicitly read their own coefficients as effective marginal rates and find the tax term larger for the income shock than for the unemployment shock in all nineteen EU countries and the United States. The marginal-versus-average asymmetry is thus a known, universal feature, and we do not claim it---nor, now, do we claim it dominates. What is new here is threefold. First, the \emph{contingency result}: the two margins are cushioned to a similar degree, but only one of the two totals depends on claiming behaviour, and the component that does not move is exactly the one the intensive margin relies on. That is a statement about the variance of stabilisation, not its level, and it survives any choice of take-up parameter---indeed it is strongest when stated as the derivative of the total with respect to take-up, which is roughly $+7$ points per unit for displacement and identically zero for wage cuts. Second, the decomposition here is exact and residual-free, computed on the population \emph{actually displaced} by an identified shock rather than on a proportional shock applied to everyone---so the average rates in the denominator are the average rates of real manufacturing workers, not of the workforce. Third, the gate-by-gate audit: we do not infer that UC fails to reach part of the displaced population, we count which constraint binds on each family, and report that the joint partner-income taper accounts for the largest share of the failures, modelled non-claiming for the next, and the capital test---the mechanism an earlier draft asserted---for none on these data.

The corrected reading of the headline result is therefore: the tax--benefit system cushions displacement and earnings-equivalent wage cuts to a similar degree, but it does so with instruments of very different reliability---an income tax that relieves marginal earnings at marginal rates automatically, against a means-tested benefit that must be cleared and claimed. The qualitative claim that survives from the earlier draft is that in-work tax relief reaches every wage-cut worker automatically while out-of-work support reaches roughly half of displaced families and only after two gates; the claim that the totals differ by eight points, and the specific attribution of the shortfall to the capital means test, do not.

\subsection{Distributional incidence by adjustment margin}\label{sec:results-margins}

Figure~\ref{fig:decile-margins} shows the mean change in household disposable income by equivalised income decile under the three original margins, full schedule, Monte Carlo means over the 50 draws with $\pm 1$ SD bands. Two profiles emerge. The displacement margin is top-half concentrated: mean losses are largest in deciles 7--10 (\pounds 65--171 per person per year, with draw-to-draw SDs of \pounds 39--131 in those deciles---individual draws are lumpy, reflecting which households happen to contain the drawn workers) and small in the bottom four deciles (under \pounds 18). The wage-cut margin is smooth and rises broadly with income, from \pounds 6 in the bottom decile to \pounds 116 at the top (the profile is not monotone---it dips slightly at deciles 2, 8 and 9, reflecting the industry composition of those deciles' earners---but the gradient across the distribution is unambiguous): exposed manufacturing earnings sit disproportionately in the upper-middle of the distribution, and proportional cuts scale with earnings.

This decile profile yields a named finding: \emph{the tariff shock is regionally, not vertically, concentrated}. Displacement losses land hardest on deciles 7--10---manufacturing employees are mostly not poor---so the aggregate inequality and poverty statistics barely move. The Gini coefficient of equivalised disposable income rises by $0.00017 \pm 0.00017$ under full displacement (from a baseline of 0.309), and actually \emph{falls} very slightly ($-0.00011$) under the wage-cut margin, since proportional cuts to above-median earnings are mildly equalising. Relative BHC poverty rises by $0.047 \pm 0.016$ percentage points under full displacement---roughly 32{,}000 people---and by essentially zero under the wage-cut margin: a several-per-cent wage cut pushes almost no exposed household across the poverty line, while a job loss pushes some. Absolute BHC poverty against a fixed baseline line (computed manually at seed 0; Appendix~\ref{app:mc}) moves nearly identically ($+0.058$ points full displacement, $+0.029$ EPD, zero under wage cuts), confirming that the relative-line result is not an artefact of a shock-shifted median.

The near-null national headcount conceals an intensive margin that the poverty \emph{gap} makes visible. The aggregate relative BHC poverty gap---the weighted sum of household shortfalls below the line---rises by \pounds 0.125 billion a year $\pm$ 0.037 under full-schedule displacement (\pounds 0.103 billion $\pm$ 0.056 under the EPD) on a national baseline gap of \pounds 36.3 billion: a 0.3 per cent movement, invisible nationally, and only \pounds 0.014 billion (deterministic) under the wage-cut margin. But essentially the entire gap increase is borne inside \emph{affected} households (those containing a displaced worker; 20 draws, seeds 0--19): their own baseline poverty gap of \pounds 0.009 billion rises fifteen-fold to \pounds 0.134 billion post-shock, and the relative BHC poverty rate among the roughly 94{,}000 people in those households jumps from $4 \pm 6$ to $38 \pm 14$ per cent---a rise of $34 \pm 12$ percentage points ($29 \pm 11$ points under the EPD). Among the 4.6 million people in wage-cut-affected households, by contrast, the poverty rate does not move at all. The correct reading of the ``+0.047 points'' headline is therefore not that the shock is poverty-irrelevant, but that a full-year displacement is close to poverty-\emph{certain} for the small population it hits, while remaining invisible in national statistics. The distributional action, such as it is, therefore runs along the margin (who loses work versus who loses wages) and along geography (Section~\ref{sec:results-regions})---not down the income distribution.

The inactivity margin is substantively distinct from displacement: displaced over-50s transition to economic inactivity \emph{and} their Universal Credit includes the LCWRA health element (Section~\ref{sec:method-margins}), under the stated upper-bound assumption that all of them pass the Work Capability Assessment. The three-way comparison under the full schedule (all margins now on the same 50 draws) is: cushioning rates of $38.7 \pm 4.1$ per cent (displacement), $39.8 \pm 4.2$ per cent (inactivity) and 41.0 per cent (wage cut); Exchequer costs of \pounds 0.92 billion $\pm$ 0.31 (displacement), \pounds 0.94 billion $\pm$ 0.32 (inactivity) and \pounds 0.99 billion (wage cut); relative BHC poverty changes of $+0.047 \pm 0.016$, $+0.043 \pm 0.015$ and $0.00$ percentage points. The inactivity--displacement contrast is a paired-seeds statement, and the pairing is now unambiguous: on common seeds the health element makes inactivity \emph{more} cushioned and \emph{more} expensive than displacement---by about \pounds 22 million a year, positive in 50 of 50 paired draws under the full schedule and 50 of 50 under the EPD (\pounds 19 million)---and the cushioning rate is higher in every one of the 50 paired draws. The wedge is small because the element pays only where UC is actually in payment: roughly $12{,}400$ of the $34{,}200$ displaced are 50 or over and flagged, but about half of displaced families clear no UC gate (Section~\ref{sec:results-mechanism}), so many LCWRA elements attach to awards that are already zero. Poverty is essentially unchanged (marginally \emph{lower} under inactivity in 20 of 50 draws, never higher), and no cross-margin ordering flips: among the three earnings-equivalent families displacement remains the least cushioned and cheapest, wage cuts the most cushioned and most expensive, and benefit-financed inactivity sits strictly between (the reallocation family of Section~\ref{sec:results-reallocation} removes a smaller gross loss, so it is comparable on rates but not on levels). A sensitivity variant in which only half the displaced over-50s pass the Work Capability Assessment thins the flag by construction and roughly halves the fiscal wedge; because the wedge is under \pounds 25 million against Exchequer costs near \pounds 0.9 billion, no reported ordering depends on it. The remaining caveats are assessment realism---no WCA process, waiting period or conditionality regime is modelled---and the upper-bound qualification (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}); UK history says the margin matters \citep{beatty2007, beatty2017}, and the fiscal wedge quantified here is its first-round price.

\subsection{Who bears it: gender, age and household type}\label{sec:results-demographics}

Tables~\ref{tab:demog} and~\ref{tab:hhtype} report who the displaced are and how much of their loss the state absorbs, over 20 Monte Carlo draws (seeds 0--19; full schedule). Group cushioning rates attribute each household's disposable-income change to its affected workers in proportion to their gross earnings losses, so the rows aggregate exactly to the economy-wide rates of Table~\ref{tab:cushioning}.

Three patterns are worth naming, with the standing Monte Carlo discipline that group-level draws are lumpy. First, gender: $73 \pm 9$ per cent of the displaced are men---exposed manufacturing is male-dominated---but the system cushions displaced women \emph{less} ($32 \pm 7$ against $40 \pm 5$ per cent), a one-sided contrast in almost every draw even though the means sit within one joint SD. Under the wage-cut margin the gender gap in cushioning nearly closes (40.4 versus 41.2 per cent): the asymmetry is a property of the gated out-of-work system, not of the tax schedule. This is the tax--benefit-system counterpart of the \citet{delyonpessoa2021} finding that UK women bore the China shock disproportionately through lost employment years: here, when women do lose manufacturing jobs, the state also replaces less of the loss. Second, age: displacement is concentrated in prime age (30 and 25 per cent of the displaced are 35--44 and 45--54 respectively), and cushioning is weakest at the two ends---$29 \pm 9$ per cent for under-25s and $26 \pm 12$ per cent for the 65-plus---the groups least likely to clear UC's gates. Third, household type, which is where the mechanism section's partner-income taper becomes visible in the incidence: half the displaced ($50 \pm 14$ per cent) live in dual-earner couples, and their cushioning rate ($35.9 \pm 5.3$ per cent) sits below single-earner couples' ($42.3 \pm 6.7$)---the taper gate at work, and now a wider gap than the two means' standard deviations would suggest is accidental, though I still flag rather than lean on the pairwise ordering. The cleaner statement is again the cross-margin one: under wage cuts, where UC is largely irrelevant, dual- and single-earner couples are cushioned almost identically (42.0 versus 43.1 per cent); the differential opens only on the displacement margin, where the joint means test operates.

\subsection{Fiscal incidence}\label{sec:results-fiscal}

Table~\ref{tab:fiscal} reports the annual Exchequer cost---foregone tax plus additional benefit spending---by scenario, and Figure~\ref{fig:exchequer-draws} shows the full draw distribution. Under the full schedule, displacement costs \pounds 0.92 billion $\pm$ 0.31 (mean $\pm$ SD over 50 draws) and the wage-cut margin \pounds 0.99 billion; under the EPD, \pounds 0.70 billion $\pm$ 0.34 and \pounds 0.77 billion. The two margins now cost the Exchequer almost the same, which is the fiscal mirror of the corrected cushioning result: the state absorbs wage cuts automatically at marginal income-tax rates for every affected worker, and absorbs job losses through a smaller tax offset topped up by Universal Credit, arriving at a similar total by a different route. MC discipline applies with force here: the displacement--wage-cut gap of \pounds 0.07 billion (full schedule; \pounds 0.06 billion EPD) is a fifth of the \pounds 0.31 billion draw-to-draw standard deviation, and the deterministic wage-cut cost exceeds the displacement draw in 34 of 50 draws under the full schedule and 36 of 50 under the EPD---not identified, and not asserted. Orderings that clearly survive the SD: every scenario costs the Exchequer strictly more than zero. The full schedule costs more than the EPD within each margin in expectation, but this too is a paired-draws statement: the full$-$EPD difference on common seeds averages \pounds 0.21 billion $\pm$ 0.39 and is positive in 37 of 50 paired draws---directionally consistent, though individual draws can reverse it.

Whichever margin, the fiscal incidence dwarfs the poverty incidence. Set against a poverty rise of at most five-hundredths of a percentage point, an annual Exchequer cost of \pounds 0.7--1.0 billion says the 2025 tariff reaches the UK household sector primarily as a public-finance problem, mediated by the automatic stabilisers, rather than as a poverty problem.

\subsection{The EPD counterfactual}\label{sec:results-epd}

Table~\ref{tab:epd} and Figure~\ref{fig:epd} price the Economic Prosperity Deal as the difference between the two tariff scenarios run through identical machinery. On the displacement margin the deal averts about $6{,}500$ job losses: that is the mean paired-draw difference over the 50 Monte Carlo displacement draws ($34{,}211$ versus $27{,}700$, $\pm 4{,}344$, positive in 47 of 50 paired draws), and it is the figure this paper quotes throughout. The corresponding quota expectation---the analytic head-count target before any draw is realised---is $6{,}857$ ($33{,}566$ versus $26{,}709$). The deal also averts \pounds 0.38 billion of gross annual earnings loss (\pounds 1.77 versus \pounds 1.39 billion), and roughly \pounds 0.2 billion of annual Exchequer cost (\pounds 0.21 billion $\pm$ 0.39 mean paired-draw difference on the displacement margin, positive in 37 of 50 paired draws; \pounds 0.22 billion, deterministic, on the wage-cut margin). Three sectoral clauses do all the work. The auto rate cut from 25 to the in-quota 10 per cent more than halves the largest single-sector shock, and because the 100{,}000-vehicle quota approximately equals 2024 US export volumes, the in-quota rate is the operative one. The pharmaceutical exemption removes the SIC 21 employment shock entirely---by construction, since the exemption's price was paid elsewhere (Section~\ref{sec:policy-pharma}). Steel relief is modelled as partial (half the 25 per cent rate), and carries the standing caveat that with roughly 80 per cent of UK steel exports going to the EU, the US-tariff cell understates the sector's true trade-policy exposure, which is dominated by EU/UK safeguards.

\subsection{Regional incidence}\label{sec:results-regions}

The regional profile is where the shock's concentration actually lives, and it moves with the margin. The main-text geography is the ITL1/government office region level, because that is the level at which the FRS records region as an \emph{observed} variable; the map is Figure~\ref{fig:regionmaps} and the full three-scenario table is Table~\ref{tab:regions}, both in Appendix~\ref{app:regions}. A constituency-level projection is reported alongside them, as an explicitly illustrative exhibit (Appendix~\ref{app:constituency})---it rests on imputed industry codes and cannot reproduce observed local industry mix, so it is not carried in the main text. All regional displacement figures below are Monte Carlo means $\pm$ SD over the 50 draws. Under full-schedule displacement the largest mean regional losses are Wales (\pounds 105 $\pm$ 225 per person per year---the enormous SD reflects a single high-weight Welsh record that dominates the draws it enters), the East Midlands (\pounds 68 $\pm$ 70, reflecting machinery, transport-equipment and food-manufacturing employment), the East of England (\pounds 64 $\pm$ 69), the West Midlands (\pounds 64 $\pm$ 55) and Scotland (\pounds 58 $\pm$ 47, with beverages, i.e.\ Scotch whisky at a 6.3 per cent US-export intensity, prominent). No pairwise regional ordering survives one draw-to-draw SD, and I do not assert one. Under the deterministic wage-cut margin the same aggregate loss spreads smoothly: Wales (\pounds 65), the East of England (\pounds 61) and the West Midlands (\pounds 55) lead, because the proportional cut weights regions by their exposed \emph{wage bills} rather than by where the discrete displacement draw lands. Under EPD displacement the mean profile is led by Wales (\pounds 74 $\pm$ 172), the East Midlands (\pounds 66 $\pm$ 84) and Northern Ireland (\pounds 56 $\pm$ 48); Yorkshire and the Humber, the steel region for which the deal's conditional half-relief is the only mitigation, averages \pounds 33 $\pm$ 35---though its worst single draw reaches \pounds 159 under the EPD (and \pounds 218 under the full schedule), which illustrates exactly how far one draw can sit from the mean. The concentration signature is therefore a statement about \emph{within-draw} geography: in any single draw the displacement losses pile up in a handful of regions---which is why the region-level SDs are comparable to or larger than the means---while wage cuts spread thinly everywhere. That is the margin's geographic signature, and it is the regional counterpart of the Gini result: a shock that is invisible in national inequality statistics can still be a \pounds 100--200-per-head event in whichever regions a given realisation hits.

\subsection{The reallocation margin}\label{sec:results-reallocation}

The three families of Section~\ref{sec:method-margins} leave a gap that the China-shock literature fills empirically: displaced manufacturing workers do not, for the most part, stay out of work. They move into services---health and social care, retail, delivery and land transport, food service---at a wage penalty \citep{autor2014, dauth2021, traiberman2019}. \citet{autor2014} track US workers' cumulative earnings after trade exposure and find the losses run through re-employment at lower pay rather than through permanent non-employment; \citet{dauth2021} and \citet{traiberman2019} document the same reallocation-with-penalty pattern in German and Danish administrative data. A margin grid that prices only job loss, an in-place wage cut and inactivity therefore omits the case the evidence actually describes. This subsection adds it: a fourth family in which the \emph{same} drawn workers as the displacement family keep a job, but in services and at a calibrated earnings penalty.

\paragraph{Calibration.} The penalty is measured on the FRS 2024--25 itself at period 2026, grossing-weighted over employees with positive earnings. The source population is the exposed goods-producing divisions SIC 10--32---mean annual earnings \pounds 48{,}272 over 2{,}047 annual hours, \pounds 23.58 an hour. The destination population is the four services divisions the literature identifies as absorbing displaced manufacturing labour---SIC 47 retail, 86 human health, 49 land transport (including delivery and couriers) and 56 food and beverage service---mean annual earnings \pounds 34{,}594 over 1{,}701 hours, \pounds 20.34 an hour. The ratio implies a \textbf{28.3 per cent annual-earnings penalty}, and it deliberately embeds the hours fall (1{,}701 against 2{,}047), because annual earnings, not the hourly wage, is what enters household income and the tax--benefit calculation. Controlling crudely for hours and age (weighted OLS of log annual earnings on a destination dummy, log hours, age and age squared over the same population) leaves a \textbf{14.0 per cent} pure hourly-wage penalty---the unconditional hourly gap is 13.8 per cent---and that figure is run as an explicit low-penalty variant rather than buried as a footnote. ASHE 2024 cross-checks the range: full-time median gross weekly pay is about \pounds 700 in manufacturing against \pounds 590 in retail, \pounds 650 in transport and storage, \pounds 670 in health and \pounds 490 in food service, a 10--20 per cent full-time-only gap that brackets the hours-controlled figure. The FRS all-employee number is larger precisely because it also captures the shift into part-time work, which the full-time-only ASHE comparison excludes by construction. Destination assignment is stochastic in proportion to FRS employee-weight shares within the four divisions: health 45.8 per cent, retail 29.5, food service 15.5, land transport 9.2. A variant places workers out of work for three months before the services job starts, scaling annual earnings and hours by a further $1 - 3/12$.

\paragraph{Results.} Table~\ref{tab:reallocation} sets the four families side by side under the full schedule, with the lag and low-penalty variants. The reallocation runs use 20 draws with seeds $0,\dots,19$, \emph{shared with the displacement and inactivity runs}, so every reallocation--displacement comparison is paired draw-for-draw: within a given seed the same workers are hit in every family, $33{,}566$ weighted on average across the 20 draws.

On the \textbf{cushioning rate}---the scale-free object---reallocation now sits \emph{above} both poles rather than between them: $0.3892$ (displacement) $< 0.4102$ (wage cut) $< 0.4482$ (reallocation). The correction to the take-up treatment is what moved it: displacement rose to meet the wage cut, and reallocation, whose movers keep an earnings stream and are relieved partly through in-work UC tapers as well as marginal income tax, sits above both. The ordering remains monotone in time spent out of work: inserting a three-month jobless spell pulls the reallocation rate back towards the poles ($0.4222$), and halving the penalty pushes it further up ($0.5070$, on a small denominator and with a correspondingly wide SD). The mechanism is the one of Section~\ref{sec:results-mechanism}: a worker who keeps an earnings stream is relieved at marginal income-tax rates and through in-work Universal Credit tapers without passing an out-of-work gate, while a worker who loses the job entirely is relieved at average rates and must clear the gated extensive margin. Under the EPD schedule the ordering is the same: $0.3779$ (displacement) $< 0.3961$ (lag-3) $< 0.4079$ (wage cut) $< 0.4143$ (reallocation) $< 0.4542$ (low penalty), with Exchequer costs of \pounds 0.648, 0.343, 0.216 and 0.114 billion against the wage cut's \pounds 0.769 billion, and poverty changes of $+0.033$, $+0.006$, $+0.001$ and $-0.001$ percentage points against $+0.000$.

\paragraph{An ordering that must not be over-read.} On \emph{levels} reallocation is the cheapest family in the paper, and the reason is arithmetic, not economics. The reallocation family removes only the wage penalty on the workers who move ($0.283 \times \pounds 1.823$ billion $= \pounds 0.516$ billion of gross earnings), whereas the wage-cut family is calibrated to remove the full earnings-equivalent aggregate spread across the entire division wage bill (\pounds 1.771 billion). The two families are answering different questions and their pound figures are not commensurable. Reallocation is level-comparable \emph{only} with displacement, where the worker sets and the draws are identical; against the wage cut it is a comparison of rates, not of costs. I state the honest summary directly: \emph{conditional on the same workers being hit, landing a services job at the FRS-measured penalty removes about two-thirds of the fiscal cost and all of the poverty impact of job loss.} The paired-draw statistics make that precise. Relative to displacement on common seeds, reallocation lowers the Exchequer cost by \pounds 0.566 billion (SE of the paired difference 0.041), lowers the relative BHC poverty rise by $0.052$ percentage points (SE $0.005$) and raises the cushioning rate by $0.059$ (SE $0.012$); with a three-month lag the differences are $-\pounds 0.394$ billion (SE 0.031), $-0.047$ percentage points (SE 0.004) and $+0.033$ (SE 0.007). Under the EPD they are $-\pounds 0.432$ billion (SE 0.044), $-0.032$ percentage points (SE 0.003) and $+0.036$ (SE 0.010) for reallocation, and $-\pounds 0.305$ billion (SE 0.034), $-0.027$ percentage points (SE 0.003) and $+0.018$ (SE 0.007) with the lag. Every one of these is many standard errors from zero---the pairing on shared seeds removes almost all of the draw noise that makes the level SDs so large.

\paragraph{What the model does and does not price.} A second caveat deserves the same plainness. The sector switch is literal: each reallocated worker's \texttt{sic\_industry\_division} is reassigned to one of the four destinations, and the assignment is reported. But \texttt{sic\_industry\_division} is a pure input variable in PolicyEngine UK---nothing downstream in the tax--benefit calculation reads it. The destination therefore carries no fiscal content of its own, and \emph{all} of the economic content of this family comes from the earnings and hours penalty applied to the mover. Nothing here should be read as the model pricing the sector of re-employment; it prices a calibrated earnings cut on a set of workers who would otherwise have been unemployed.

\paragraph{Inequality.} The Gini result reinforces the paper's existing scoping of distributional claims. Reallocation leaves the Gini of equivalised disposable income essentially unmoved ($-0.00003$), against $+0.00017$ under displacement. The inequality signature of this shock, small as it already is, is a property of the \emph{displacement} margin specifically---of workers leaving the earnings distribution altogether---and not of the tariff shock as such. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} scopes the two structural distributional claims accordingly.

\subsection{Validation against the 2025--26 outturn}\label{sec:results-validation}

The design lets the realised data discipline the one free parameter. ONS monthly trade data show UK goods exports to the US falling 24.7 per cent in April 2025, the first full tariff month; against the 12.8 per cent trade-weighted average tariff implied by the primitives in Table~\ref{tab:shocks}, this pins the export-demand elasticity at 1.93, which I round to 2.0. The calibration is deliberately an \emph{outturn} anchor rather than a literature import---but it shares April's observation, so it validates nothing by itself. The out-of-sample test uses the ten subsequent months. From the same HMRC customs source as the intensity build, pulled monthly (Appendix~\ref{app:data}), I compute each division's realised export fall over May 2025 to February 2026 against the mean of the same calendar months one and two years earlier---same-month comparisons net out seasonality, and the two-year average dilutes the front-running contamination of the one-year base; April 2025, the calibration month, is excluded.

Because the paper quotes both a 24.7 and a 12.7 per cent fall, the relation between them should be stated plainly rather than left to the reader: they are different statistics, not competing estimates, and nothing here contradicts the ONS. The 24.7 is an April 2025 month-on-month fall against the front-run March peak; the 12.7 is a ten-month window against a two-year same-calendar-month baseline. Computed on the ONS's own month-on-month basis, our data give 21.7 per cent for April (\pounds 4.85 billion to \pounds 3.80 billion), with the remaining three points accounted for by the HMRC customs versus ONS balance-of-payments basis---our 2024 mapped total is \pounds 52.5 billion of a \pounds 55.6 billion all-commodity customs total against an ONS balance-of-payments figure of about \pounds 59.3 billion---rather than by any difference in the underlying movement. Both series exclude non-monetary gold and the other precious metals, and that exclusion is load-bearing: on the same April calculation, retaining them would report a 44.2 per cent fall, which is exactly the bullion-driven artefact the exclusion exists to remove (Appendix~\ref{app:data}). Nor is the window result an artefact of baseline construction---every post-window month falls in the \pounds 3.34--4.22 billion range against a 2024 monthly range of \pounds 4.07--4.83 billion, so every post month sits below every 2024 equivalent.

Table~\ref{tab:validation} sets the $\varepsilon$-model's predicted export falls ($\varepsilon\tau_j$, full and EPD schedules) against the realised falls for the ten divisions with the largest derived full-schedule shocks. Three honest readings. First, the \emph{aggregate} holds up reasonably: mapped US exports fell 12.7 per cent over the window, about half the April collapse persisting once the EPD (in force for autos from late June 2025) and the post-April rebound are in the data. Against trade-weighted aggregate predictions of 25.1 per cent (full schedule) and 17.4 per cent (EPD schedule), the realised 12.7 sits below both but far nearer the EPD---the right ordering for an outturn that embeds the deal, with the residual gap the footprint of rebound and re-routing that a static demand curve does not capture. The autos-led profile is confirmed: motor vehicles show the largest realised fall among the schedule-differentiated divisions (32.0 per cent, against a predicted 50 under the full schedule and 20 under the EPD)---though that figure is baseline-dependent in the same way as pharmaceuticals below: on a calendar-2025-versus-2024 baseline the car fall is 27.4 per cent, and the ONS reports 24.4, so the two-year baseline, which deliberately dilutes the front-run base, is itself the main source of the gap to the published figure. That is a defensible choice for a measure meant to capture the tariff rather than the anticipation of it, but it does deepen the measured fall and should be read as such. Second, the \emph{cross-section} is where the model is weakest, and the table says so: the Spearman rank correlation between predicted and realised falls across the top ten is $0.00$ under the full schedule and, on the adversarial read that deserves the main text rather than a table note, $-0.62$ under the EPD: across the ten most exposed divisions the deal-mitigated schedule ranks them not merely uninformatively but backwards. The failure is informative about channels the tariff schedule does not encode. Pharmaceuticals fell \emph{despite} the eventual exemption---the Section 232 threat and the unwinding of the enormous Q1-2025 stockpiling operated on a division the EPD schedule scores at zero---though the size of that fall is the most baseline-sensitive number in the paper and is better reported as a span than a point. The crosswalk is exact here (division 21 is SITC 54, identical to the ONS's ``medicinal and pharmaceutical products''), so the divergence from the ONS's published 16.2 per cent is not a mapping problem but a baseline choice: our own data give 10.9 per cent on a calendar-2025-versus-2024 basis and 30.7 per cent on the two-year-average baseline, with the ONS figure sitting inside that range. The honest statement is a realised pharma fall of 10.9 to 30.7 per cent, with 30.7---the figure that enters the measured family---the upper end of that span rather than the estimate. Wearing apparel fell 28.9 per cent on a flat 10 per cent tariff. Exports \emph{rose} in aerospace ($+9.8$ per cent; the EPD's aerospace carve-out, which the stylised schedule leaves at 10 per cent), in basic metals ($+11.2$ per cent) and in electrical equipment. Third, at the level that actually enters the microsimulation---the earnings shock $s_j$, which multiplies the fall by the export \emph{intensity}---the full-schedule model and the measured build agree better: Spearman $0.58$ across all 23 divisions, because the intensity term, which is measured rather than predicted, carries most of the cross-sectional variance. The fair summary is that the one-parameter model gets the aggregate and the autos signature right and the within-manufacturing ranking wrong; the measured family of Section~\ref{sec:results-measured} therefore replaces the prediction with the outturn wholesale.

The basic-metals rise deserves separating out, because it is the one apparent anomaly in the table that turns, on inspection, into a substantive point in the model's favour. The $+11.2$ per cent is not a precious-metals artefact---non-monetary gold and the silver and platinum group are excluded throughout (Appendix~\ref{app:data})---but a composition effect within a division that mixes tariffed and untariffed products. Inside division 24 every ferrous steel group moves as the tariff predicts: SITC 671 falls 21 per cent, 672 by 20, 673 by 60, 674 by 5, 677 by 19 and 679 by 27. The division-level rise comes entirely from non-ferrous metals lying outside the steel tariff lines---copper (682) up 72 per cent, lead (685) up 134, and alloy and wire products (675, 676) up 105 and 37 respectively---with copper separately front-run ahead of its own Section 232 action. The division therefore averages a tariffed steel collapse with an untariffed and partly anticipatory non-ferrous surge, and the division-level figure \emph{understates} the steel shock rather than contradicting it. We nevertheless do not adjust it, for a unit-of-analysis reason: the division is the level at which employment counts and the FRS crosswalk are defined, so a sub-division reweighting of the export fall would have no matching denominator on the employment side and would silently mix units. A similar check clears the other manufacturing division: SIC 32's realised fall is not precious-metal-driven either, since SITC 897 (articles of precious metal) is only \pounds 187 million of post-window flow and itself falls 24.6 per cent.

Which \emph{adjustment margin} the data chose remains harder to read, and this paper does not resolve it. The natural instrument is the ONS Business Insights and Conditions Survey, whose fortnightly waves record whether exposed firms report price and margin adjustment, workforce reduction, or neither; matching those responses to the exposed SIC divisions over the 2025--26 window would discriminate between the wage-cut and displacement families directly. That exercise is left to further work: it requires a wave-by-wave build the present pipeline does not contain, and we prefer to leave the margin an explicit scenario dimension than to assert a reading of the survey we have not conducted.

\subsection{The measured family: the realised outturn as the shock}\label{sec:results-measured}

The validation table invites the obvious next step: discard the elasticity entirely and let the realised outturn \emph{be} the shock. The measured family sets $s_j^{\text{measured}} = \max(\text{fall}_j, 0) \times x_j$---each division's realised export fall times its US-export intensity, with divisions whose exports rose taking a zero shock---so no elasticity, no tariff rate and no pass-through-to-demand assumption enter; the export-side demand response, EPD mitigation included, is read off the data. Each fall is a proportional rate---one minus the ratio of the post-tariff window to the baseline window over the same ten calendar months (May to February) in different years, so seasonality nets out and the quantity is dimensionless rather than a partial-year level---applied as if it persisted over a full year, since the microsimulation is annual, and imposed on the 2026 tax--benefit system and 2026 uprated incomes. Because the outturn embeds the deal, this family has a single tariff scenario rather than a full/EPD pair; it is run under the displacement (20 draws) and wage-cut margins with the machinery of Section~\ref{sec:method-margins} unchanged, including the maintained assumption that the full export fall passes through to the sector wage bill.

The measured shock is \emph{smaller than either calibrated schedule}: the aggregate gross earnings loss is \pounds 0.95 billion a year, against \pounds 1.77 billion under the full schedule and \pounds 1.39 billion under the EPD. That it lands below even the EPD is the validation table's cross-section translated into pounds: the divisions where the calibrated model over-predicts (aerospace, steel, electrical equipment, computers---realised falls near zero or negative) carry more of the exposed wage bill than the divisions where it under-predicts (apparel, pharmaceuticals), and the clipping of realised export \emph{rises} to zero shocks removes loss the model imputes. Read against the calibrated families, the outturn says the first post-tariff year delivered roughly half the full-schedule earnings shock---the EPD working as priced on autos, but also rebound, re-routing and resilience the one-parameter demand curve does not capture. The implied headline results scale accordingly: displacement puts $17{,}700 \pm 3{,}100$ workers out of work (against $34{,}200$ full, $27{,}700$ EPD), costs the Exchequer \pounds 0.41 billion $\pm$ 0.22 a year (wage-cut margin: \pounds 0.53 billion, deterministic), and raises relative BHC poverty by $0.025 \pm 0.013$ percentage points (wage cut: zero).

The structural findings survive the change of shock family intact, which is the point of running it. The cushioning pattern reappears with the same shape: the system absorbs $37.5 \pm 5.2$ per cent of the measured displacement loss across 50 draws (38.8 per cent at seed 0) against 41.6 per cent of the earnings-equivalent wage cut---a difference of the same modest size, inside the same draw noise, produced by the same opposition of instruments, on a differently-shaped sector shock. The Gini moves by $+0.00012$ (displacement) and $-0.00007$ (wage cut); poverty is again a displacement-margin phenomenon only. The measured family thus separates the paper's two kinds of claim: the pound-denominated \emph{levels} depend on the shock family and are roughly halved relative to the full schedule---readers should treat the measured levels as the current best estimate of the realised first-year shock---while the \emph{structural} findings this family can test---the two margins cushioned similarly but by opposite instruments, and fiscal incidence dwarfing poverty incidence---are properties of the tax--benefit rules that the change of shock family does not overturn. The measured shock was not run under the inactivity or reallocation margins, and no measured regional exhibit is reported, so those orderings are established only for the calibrated families.
