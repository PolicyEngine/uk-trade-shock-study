\begin{abstract}
\noindent This paper quantifies how much of a trade-related labour-income shock the UK tax--benefit system absorbs, and how the answer changes when the same sectoral loss arrives through wage cuts, job displacement, inactivity or reallocation. Using Family Resources Survey households and PolicyEngine UK, it provides a transparent statutory fiscal-incidence stress test calibrated to UK exposure to the 2025 US tariffs. Under the full schedule, the imposed direct earnings stress is \pounds0.886 billion annually; the system offsets 41.3 per cent when the loss is delivered as wage cuts and 34.5 per cent under displacement. The exercise is static, partial-equilibrium and first-round: it does not estimate tariff pass-through, production, productivity, labour demand, prices or total welfare. The tariff-to-wage-bill bridge and adjustment margins are imposed scenarios, not causal estimates. Assignment dispersion is reported separately from numerical Monte Carlo uncertainty. The contribution is therefore deliberately narrow: showing how adjustment margins reshape UK fiscal and distributional incidence conditional on a common labour-income loss.
\end{abstract}
