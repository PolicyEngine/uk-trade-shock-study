\begin{abstract}
\noindent I study who bears the 2025 US tariffs on UK goods after the tax--benefit system responds. The shock enters through primitives: the tariff schedule (10 per cent baseline, 25 per cent autos and steel, later mitigated by the Economic Prosperity Deal) is combined with US-export intensity by SIC division---built from HMRC trade-by-SIC and ONS turnover data---and an export-demand elasticity of 2.0, anchored on the realised outturn (US-bound goods exports fell 24.7 per cent in April 2025 against a 12.8 per cent trade-weighted tariff), to derive sector-level earnings shocks passed through PolicyEngine UK on Family Resources Survey 2024--25 microdata. Employment is an outcome of the derived shock, not a free input. Holding the aggregate gross earnings loss fixed (\pounds 1.8 billion a year, 0.06 per cent of GDP on the direct channel), I vary the \emph{adjustment margin} across four families: job loss, wage cuts in place, exit into economic inactivity, and literal reallocation of the same displaced workers into services (retail, health, land transport, food service) at an FRS-calibrated 28.3 per cent annual-earnings penalty. The headline finding reverses the naive prior: the tax--benefit system cushions in-work wage cuts (41 per cent) \emph{more} than earnings-equivalent job losses (33 per cent), and an exact component decomposition demonstrates why: income tax relieves wage cuts at marginal rates but job losses only at average rates (35 versus 20 pence per pound of gross loss), while Universal Credit reaches only a third of displaced workers' families, gated by modelled take-up and the joint partner-income taper. The reallocation margin falls exactly between the two poles on the cushioning rate (34.5 per cent displacement, 39.2 reallocation, 41.0 wage cut, monotone in time spent out of work), and on paired draws over identical worker sets, landing a services job at the calibrated penalty removes about two-thirds of the fiscal cost and roughly nine-tenths of the poverty impact of job loss. The shock reaches households mainly as a fiscal event---\pounds 0.7--1.0 billion a year to the Exchequer (Monte Carlo means over 50 displacement draws, draw-to-draw standard deviation \pounds 0.3 billion) against a poverty rise of at most 0.06 percentage points---and, on the displacement margin, is regionally rather than vertically concentrated: losses land in deciles 7--10, and while national inequality statistics barely move, the hardest-hit regions average \pounds 60--110 per person per year across draws, with any single realisation concentrating \pounds 100--200 per head on a few regions. Two results deserve emphasis beyond the headline. First, a measured-outturn family that discards the elasticity entirely and lets the realised 2025--26 export falls \emph{be} the shock---the only estimate in the paper with no free parameter---puts the gross earnings loss at \pounds 0.95 billion a year, roughly half the calibrated full-schedule figure, while leaving every structural ordering intact. Second, the poverty result is a general warning about measurement: national relative poverty rises by just 0.055 percentage points, but among the households actually hit the poverty rate goes from 4 to 43 per cent and their poverty gap rises twenty-fold---a concentrated shock that national headcount statistics are structurally unable to register. The Economic Prosperity Deal averted roughly 6{,}500 job losses (Monte Carlo mean over 50 paired displacement draws, $\pm 4{,}388$, positive in 47 of 50; the analytic quota expectation is 6{,}857), \pounds 0.4 billion of gross earnings and roughly \pounds 0.2 billion a year of Exchequer cost (positive in 36 of 50 paired draws).
\end{abstract}
