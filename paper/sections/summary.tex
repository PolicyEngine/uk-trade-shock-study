\section*{Executive summary}

This paper estimates a conditional fiscal-incidence response, not the causal
economic effect of a tariff. Under the full schedule, the imposed direct
earnings stress is \pounds0.886 billion per year. When the same aggregate loss
is realised as a wage cut, the tax--benefit system offsets 41.3 per cent and
the Exchequer cost is \pounds0.495 billion. When it is realised as displacement,
the central ten-assignment artifact offsets 34.5\,per cent, costs
\pounds0.388\,billion, and raises relative BHC poverty by 0.014 percentage
points. These figures are conditional on the imposed stress and adjustment
margin; they are not tariff forecasts.

The policy implication is narrow but actionable: automatic tax relief is
available immediately after an earnings cut, whereas protection after job loss
depends on benefit-unit eligibility, take-up, and the assumed inactivity
route. Temporary wage insurance, faster benefit enrolment, and targeted work
allowances are natural counterfactuals. Their fiscal costs and distributional
effects must be evaluated with the same rules-based engine and explicit
financing assumptions.
