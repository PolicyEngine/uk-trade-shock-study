# Literature review: "Who bears the tariff?" — the 2025 US–UK trade shock through the tax–benefit system

Working notes for the tariff-incidence companion paper. Compiled July 2026.

## 0. The two anchor papers

- **Ignatenko, Lashkaripour, Macedoni & Simonovska (2025), "Making America Great Again? The Economic Impacts of Liberation Day Tariffs," *Journal of International Economics* 157, 104138.** Quantitative GE model (nests Armington/EK/Krugman/Melitz), exact hat algebra, 194 countries. Tariffs enter as a **primitive** on trade shares; wages flexible; elastic labor supply (κ=0.5) makes **employment an equilibrium outcome** — and its sign flips with revenue recycling (income-tax cut: US employment +0.32%; lump-sum rebate: −0.41%). Under retaliation with input–output linkages: US welfare up to −3.38%, global employment −0.58%. Optimal US tariff is a *uniform 19%* independent of bilateral deficits. UK is in the sample; replication data public (Mendeley) — can pull UK-specific welfare/employment cells under the 10% tariff. **Use for:** the structural upstream anchor; the revenue-recycling caveat on fiscal incidence.
- **Atkin, Bernadac, Donaldson, Garg & Huneeus (Dec 2025 draft), "The Incidence of Distortions."** Baqaee–Farhi wedge framework on Chilean admin data (firm-to-firm e-invoices, consumer-linked receipts, matched employer–employee). Tariffs are one wedge type; factor supplies **fixed** — no employment margin; all adjustment via flexible wages/prices; household incidence via consumption baskets, factor ownership, profits, transfers. "Statutory vs economic incidence" framing. **Use for:** household-incidence formulas; the polar wage-margin benchmark; cites tariff-incidence precedents (Faber 2014; Acosta & Cox 2024; Conlon–Rao–Wang 2022).

These two papers are the poles of the adjustment-margin question: one derives employment from the tariff primitive; the other assumes it fixed and lets wages absorb everything.

## 1. China-shock lineage (shock → labor-market incidence)

- **Autor, Dorn & Hanson (2013), "The China Syndrome," *AER* 103(6)** — Import competition caused large persistent employment/wage losses in exposed US commuting zones. The canonical shift-share reduced form: exposure in, employment as *outcome*.
- **Autor, Dorn & Hanson (2021), "On the Persistence of the China Shock," *BPEA*** — Effects persist to 2019; no migration adjustment. Justifies long-lived displacement scenarios.
- **Caliendo, Dvorkin & Parro (2019), *Econometrica* 87(3)** — Dynamic spatial GE with costly mobility; China shock as productivity/trade-cost primitive → ~0.55m US manufacturing job losses; aggregate gains, concentrated regional losses. The structural counterpart our reduced-form scenarios should be benchmarked against.
- **Adão, Arkolakis & Esposito (2019/2023, NBER w25544)** — Theory-consistent mapping from regional shift-share estimates to GE counterfactuals; caveat for interpreting regional results as aggregates.
- **Kim & Vogel (2021, AER: Insights)** — Maps reduced-form employment/wage responses into money-metric worker welfare; losses of displaced workers exceed wage losses; incidence regressive. Supports the earnings→welfare translation step.
- **Galle, Rodríguez-Clare & Yi (2023), *REStud* 90(1)** — Roy-model GE: aggregate gains coexist with group-specific losses. Our contribution: replace stylized worker groups with real households and a real tax-benefit system.

## 2. Adjustment margins: reallocation with wage losses, not permanent unemployment

- **Autor, Dorn, Hanson & Song (2014), *QJE* 129(4)** — Worker-level: margin is mostly *earnings* losses amid churn across employers/sectors.
- **Traiberman (2019), *AER* 109(12)** — Denmark: occupation-specific human capital; reallocation with persistent wage losses.
- **Dix-Carneiro (2014), *Econometrica* 82(3)** — Brazil: reallocation takes years; adjustment costs 1.4–2.7× annual wages; older workers hit hardest. Calibrates transition horizons and age gradients.
- **Dix-Carneiro & Kovak (2017), *AER* 107(10)** — Regional effects *grow* over 20 years; joint wage/employment margin.
- **Dauth, Findeisen & Suedekum (2014 JEEA; 2021 JOLE)** — Germany: workers reallocate into services/other plants with wage cuts, not permanent unemployment — the cleanest evidence for the wage-margin family.

## 3. The 2025 US tariffs and the Economic Prosperity Deal — UK analyses

Institutional detail: 10% baseline (April 2025), 25% autos/steel; EPD (8 May 2025): 10% auto tariff on a 100,000-vehicle quota (25% above), conditional steel relief; pharma 0% for 3 years (Dec 2025 deal) in exchange for VPAG rebate cut (22.5%→14.5%) and NHS pricing concessions.

- **NIESR (Dec 2024), *Implications of Higher US Tariffs for the UK Economy*** — NiGEM scenarios: lower GDP, higher inflation via FX/rates. Macro only. [numbers: scenario paths]
- **OBR EFO (Mar 2025 tariff box; Mar 2026)** — 20ppt US tariff: permanent UK GDP loss 1/3–3/4%; ~0.6% peak without retaliation. Fiscal-aggregate benchmark. [numbers]
- **Bank of England MPRs (May/Aug/Nov 2025)** — Effective US tariff on UK ≈ **9%** (vs ~18% globally); trade diversion has *lowered* UK import prices (mildly disinflationary) — supports isolating the export-demand/earnings channel. [numbers: 9% effective rate]
- **Dhingra (BoE, Oct 2025), "Tariffed with the same brush"** — Pass-through and trade-policy volatility; authority citation for the price/monetary channel held fixed.
- **CEP/LSE + VoxEU (Decision Maker Panel)** — Most UK firms expect small negative effects; volatility itself suppressed trade; US ≈ 22% of UK exports (£183bn). Disciplines scenario sizes. [numbers]
- **Commons Library CBP-10240; BEIS Committee EPD report (+ SMMT written evidence)** — Timeline and EPD terms. [numbers: rates, 100k quota]
- **Resolution Foundation (Apr 2025), *Trump Tariff Turmoil*** — Closest household-facing analysis; descriptive, not microsimulation.
- **Centre for Cities, "The uneven storm"** — City exposure: Coventry 22.1% of exports are US goods; Derby 19.9%; Telford 13.3%. [numbers]
- **City-REDI (Birmingham)** — Auto tariffs cost UK £10bn GDP over 5 years; **West Midlands bears £6.2bn (62%)**; WM auto exports to US = 51% of UK's. Regional anchor for the cars/West Midlands cell. [numbers]
- **WMCA business impacts survey (2025)** — 50% of WM firms expect turnover decline; 24% plan production cuts by end-2025. [numbers]
- **Make UK Tariffs Hub / flash survey** — 1 in 4 manufacturers report balance-sheet losses; export front-loading early 2025.
- **SMMT releases** — US vehicle exports collapsed (7,077 units Apr → 4,223 May 2025); production forecast −15% for 2025; EPD "a huge relief". [numbers: monthly units]
- **UK Steel; UKICE steel analysis** — ~80% of UK steel exports go to Europe; steel employs ~37k, half in Wales and Yorkshire/Humber; 70–80% downstream pass-through of US steel tariffs. **Caveat: the steel/Wales cell is more exposed to EU/UK safeguards than to US tariffs.** [numbers]
- **ABPI (Dec 2025); Morgan Lewis summary** — Pharma (£6.6bn US exports 2024): 0% tariffs for 3 years, paid for via VPAG/NHS pricing. **Pharma is a fiscal/pricing shock, not an employment shock — distinct incidence channel.** [numbers]
- **IFS** — No dedicated 2025-tariff distributional publication; part of the gap statement.

**Across all of the above: macro, sector, region, firm — no analysis reaches household disposable income or the tax-benefit system. The household/fiscal cell is empty.**

## 4. Realised outturn (validation data)

- **ONS, "UK trade with the United States… April 2025 to February 2026"** — Goods exports to US (ex precious metals) fell £1.5bn (**−24.7%**) in April 2025 and stayed low; car exports still below pre-tariff levels. Underlying series: UK Trade, country-by-commodity (US), monthly.
- **ONS BICS (Wave 131 onward)** — By Feb 2026, 33% of goods exporters (10+ employees) affected by US tariffs; 18.7% report additional costs. Weighted tables per wave.
- **BoE MPR Nov 2025 staff/agents analysis** — Realised effects smaller than feared; supports modest central scenarios.
- **SMMT monthly production/exports** — Highest-frequency sector outturn; rebound after EPD implementation (30 June 2025).

Use these to test *which adjustment margin the data chose*: did auto/steel employment fall, or hours and pay?

## 5. UK trade-shock precedents (elasticity sources)

- **Dhingra, Ottaviano, Sampson & Van Reenen (2016/17), *Economic Policy*** — Brexit trade costs → UK income −1.3 to −2.7% static. Template for trade-cost primitive → income.
- **Fetzer & Wang (2020/24), "Measuring the Regional Economic Cost of Brexit" (CAGE WP 1280; brexitcost.org)** — Synthetic-control district-level output losses 5–10ppt, concentrated in manufacturing/low-human-capital districts. Best UK precedent for district-level trade-shock incidence; data downloadable. [numbers]
- **Fetzer (2019), "Did Austerity Cause Brexit?," *AER* 109(11)** — Benefit cuts drove Leave support; political economy of cushioning.
- **Bloom et al. (2019, NBER w26218)** — Brexit uncertainty cut investment ~11%, productivity 2–5%; precedent for the uncertainty channel (same DMP methodology as the tariff surveys). [numbers]
- **De Lyon & Pessoa (2021), "Worker and firm responses to trade shocks: The UK–China case" (CEP DP1741; *EER*)** — **Key UK elasticity source**: UK workers in Chinese-import-competing industries lost employment years and earnings; no equally gainful re-employment; larger female earnings losses via employment years. [numbers: worker-level elasticities]
- **Dorn & Levell (2024), *Labour Economics* 91** — Synthesis of China-shock evidence including UK.
- **Irastorza-Fadrique, Levell & Parey (IFS WP 24/52; *JIE* 2025), "Household responses to trade shocks"** — UK households adjust via added-worker effects, later retirement, self-employment. **Closest existing work — household-level UK trade shock, but behavioural responses, not tax-benefit mechanics. Cite prominently and differentiate.** [numbers]
- **Rickard (LSE), UK China-shock and politics** — Secondary motivation.

## 6. UK labour-market adjustment: the inactivity margin

- **Beatty & Fothergill (1996; 2007 with Powell, "Twenty Years On")** — Coalfield job losses became male *economic inactivity* on incapacity benefits, not measured unemployment; incomplete recovery after 20+ years. **UK-specific correction to the US/German reallocation story: the historical UK margin was benefit-financed inactivity** → in the simulation, flows to UC-with-health-element, not just UC standard allowance.
- **Beatty & Fothergill (2017), "The impact on welfare and public finances of job loss in industrial Britain" (*Regional Studies, Regional Science*)** — 1980s–90s industrial job destruction *still* inflates the deficit via higher benefit claims and lower tax receipts. **The direct UK precedent for the fiscal-incidence question — retrospective and area-level where ours is prospective, rules-based, household-level.** [numbers: per-area fiscal costs]
- **Aragón, Rud & Toews (2018), *Labour Economics* 52** — Mine closures: partial male reallocation, female employment falls; 20+ year persistence; gender-differentiated margins. [numbers]
- **Rud, Simmons, Toews & Aragón (2022, IFS WP / IZA DP 15581), "Job displacement costs of phasing out coal"** — Earnings losses decomposed into **wage cuts on re-employment vs non-employment spells** — directly calibrates the split between the displacement and wage-cut scenario families. [numbers: decomposition]
- **DWP ad-hoc JSA statistics, SSI Redcar closure (2015)** — 2,000+ job losses traced into benefit claims — natural validation case for the steel scenario. [numbers]

## 7. Tax-benefit cushioning of earnings shocks

- **Dolls, Fuest & Peichl (2012), *JPubE* 96** — EUROMOD/TAXSIM: EU systems absorb ~38% of an income shock, **~47% of an unemployment shock** (US ~32%). Methodological ancestor — but stylized macro shocks, not trade-policy-derived.
- **Sutherland & Figari (2013), *IJM* 6(1)** — EUROMOD infrastructure and "stress-test" use; method lineage alongside PolicyEngine UK.
- **Autor, Dorn & Hanson (2013; 2019 "When Work Disappears")** — Exposed CZs saw rising disability/retirement/welfare payments, but transfers offset only a small fraction of earnings losses — US benchmark for our properly computed replacement rates.
- **Hyman (2018/2022, Census CES WP 22-05)** — TAA gains came from retraining, not transfers; active-policy counterpoint.
- **Hyman, Kovak & Leive (2024, NBER w32464)** — Wage insurance raises re-employment and earnings — a natural counterfactual reform to simulate in PolicyEngine.
- **Tax Policy Center tariff models** — Allocates *consumer-price* incidence of the US's own tariffs to tax units — the nearest tariff-microsimulation link, but the opposite channel from ours (export-demand/earnings incidence of a *partner's* tariff).

## 8. Gap statement (as of July 2026)

1. **No study passes a trade-policy shock through a full tax-benefit microsimulation** to household disposable income, poverty, and the Exchequer. Nearest neighbours: Dolls et al. (stylized shocks), TPC/PolicyEngine-US (consumer-price side of own tariffs), Irastorza-Fadrique–Levell–Parey (UK household behaviour, no tax-benefit mechanics), Beatty–Fothergill 2017 (fiscal cost, retrospective/area-level/accounting).
2. UK institutional analyses stop at macro/sector/region/firm aggregates.
3. Structural incidence models (CDP, Galle et al., Kim–Vogel, ABDGH) have no realistic fiscal system; microsimulation has no trade shock. This paper sits in the empty intersection.
4. **UK-specific elasticities exist and should replace US/German ones**: De Lyon–Pessoa (worker earnings/employment), Rud et al. (wage-vs-employment decomposition), Fetzer–Wang (district output), Aragón et al. (persistence, gender).
5. The adjustment-margin axis (displacement vs wage cuts vs benefit-financed inactivity) has first-order fiscal/distributional consequences because UC replaces unemployment far more generously than in-work wage cuts — nobody has shown this.
6. Sector nuances: pharma is a fiscal/pricing shock (VPAG/NHS), steel is more an EU-safeguards story than a US-tariff story — both absent from generic exposure treatments.

## 9. Scenario design implications

- Shock enters through **primitives**: tariff schedule × HMRC/ONS trade-by-SIC → sector export-demand shocks; earnings/employment effects disciplined by (a) De Lyon–Pessoa/ADH-style elasticities and (b) the OBR scenario / Ignatenko et al. UK cell as aggregate anchors.
- Three adjustment-margin families as the central axis: **displacement** (ADH short-run), **wage cuts with reallocation into services** (Dauth/Traiberman long-run), **transition path with an inactivity tail** (Dix-Carneiro dynamics + Beatty–Fothergill UK history, age-gradient into UC health element).
- EPD counterfactual: full tariffs vs deal-mitigated (auto quota → West Midlands; steel — flag safeguards caveat; pharma → fiscal channel paragraph, not employment).
- Validation: ONS Apr 2025–Feb 2026 outturn + BICS + SMMT monthly series to test which margin the data chose.
- Caveat from Ignatenko et al.: revenue recycling can flip aggregate employment signs — we compute first-round fiscal incidence holding fiscal policy fixed.

## 10. Additions from the July 2026 coverage audit (now cited in the paper)

**2018–19 US tariff pass-through/incidence:**
- **Amiti, Redding & Weinstein (2019), *JEP* 33(4), 187–210** — near-complete pass-through of the 2018 tariffs into US import prices; incidence on US firms/consumers.
- **Fajgelbaum, Goldberg, Kennedy & Khandelwal (2020), "The Return to Protectionism," *QJE* 135(1), 1–55** — complete pass-through; aggregate real income losses; electorally-motivated protection.
- **Cavallo, Gopinath, Neiman & Tang (2021), *AER: Insights* 3(1), 19–34** — full border pass-through, muted/slower retail pass-through.
- **Flaaen & Pierce (2019), FEDS 2019-086** — US manufacturing employment *fell* on net: input costs + retaliation outweighed protection.

**Distributional effects of trade (surveys/decompositions):**
- **Fajgelbaum & Khandelwal (2016), *QJE* 131(3), 1113–1180** — unequal gains from trade via consumption baskets (pro-poor expenditure channel).
- **Fajgelbaum & Khandelwal (2022), *Annual Review of Economics* 14, 205–228** — survey of US–China trade-war economic impacts.
- **Borusyak & Jaravel (2021), NBER w28957** — expenditure vs earnings channels of trade's distributional effects.

**Retaliation incidence (partner's tariff as export-demand shock — our channel):**
- **Waugh (2019), NBER w26353** — Chinese retaliatory tariffs depressed consumption (auto purchases) in exposed US counties.
- **Blanchard, Bown & Chor (2019), NBER w26434** — electoral fallout of retaliatory tariffs across US counties.

**Export-demand shocks and worker outcomes:**
- **Hummels, Jørgensen, Munch & Xiang (2014), *AER* 104(6), 1597–1629** — Danish matched data; instrumented firm export demand → wages by task.
- **Garin & Silvério (2024), *REStud* 91(3), 1671–1710** — Portuguese exporters pass idiosyncratic export-demand shocks into incumbent workers' wages, not layoffs — micro evidence for the wage-cut family.

**Tariff incidence via microsimulation (US side):**
- **Tax Policy Center (2025), Description of TPC's tariff models** — tariffs allocated to tax units like excise taxes in the full microsimulation model (now cited directly, not just via Atkin et al.).
- **Budget Lab at Yale (2025), "Where We Stand"** — fiscal/distributional effects of all 2025 US tariffs by decile.
- **Acosta & Cox (2024), "The Regressive Nature of the US Tariff Code" (cond. accepted QJE)** — higher statutory rates on lower-end varieties; the code is built regressively.

**2025 tariffs, 2026 assessments:**
- **Fajgelbaum & Khandelwal (2026), "Tariffs in 2025: Short-Run Impacts on the U.S. Economy," *BPEA* Spring 2026 draft** — early evidence: costs borne largely by US consumers; complements our partner-country worker-side question.

## 11. Additions from the July 2026 expansion round (now cited in the paper)

**Structural/macro anchors (lit §Trade shocks and incidence):**
- **Rodríguez-Clare, Ulate & Vasquez (2025), "The 2025 Trade War: Dynamic Impacts Across U.S. States and the Global Economy," NBER w33792** — dynamic trade/reallocation model with downward nominal wage rigidity; manufacturing employment up but total employment down (participation falls with real wages); US real income −1% by 2028. - **Michelena, Ernst & Bertin (ILO 2025, arXiv:2512.11578)** — MRIO + trade model of the 2025 escalation; employment/export losses concentrated in *targeted* countries; up to 23m global job losses in the worst scenario. *Note: user brief said "Ernst & Michelena"; actual author order is Michelena, Ernst & Bertin — key `michelena2025`.*

- **Auray, Devereux & Eyquem (2020), NBER w27460 "Trade Wars, Currency Wars"** — non-trivial consumption losses for passive trade partners (brief). *The "~0.5% partner consumption loss" figure in the brief could not be verified against the paper; prose kept generic.*

**Network propagation / local multipliers (supply-chain and direct-channel caveats; lit, methodology §primitives, discussion limitation 4):**
